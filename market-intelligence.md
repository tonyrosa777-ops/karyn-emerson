# Market Intelligence — Karyn Emerson Real Estate

*Prepared for Karyn Emerson, Jill & Co. Realty Group, Salem NH. Service area: Southern NH / Greater Derry. Research date: April 2026.*

---

## 1. Executive summary

The Southern NH residential real estate market is saturated at the agent level but surprisingly thin at the **content and design level**. Every meaningful competitor — Lisa Sevajian (soldbylsg.com / northshorepropertyshop.com), BHHS Verani, Coldwell Banker Dinsmore, Lamacchia Realty's Salem office, and Karyn's own Jill & Co. — runs the same visual template: blue-and-white palette, geometric sans-serif, agent-headshot grid, and an IDX search bar slammed over a stock home exterior. None of them own the **"MA-to-NH relocator"** search cluster, none publish genuine neighborhood guides for Salem, Windham, Derry or Londonderry, and only Sevajian discloses commission ranges. Karyn sits in the most defensible position on the board — a lifelong Southern NH operator at a genuine boutique brokerage — and the single biggest opportunity is to **own the relocation + hyperlocal-neighborhood narrative** with editorial design, transparent pricing, and a place-first (not face-first) content strategy.

The real threat is Sevajian, not the franchises. She publishes a 2–6% commission range on her site, posts weekly long-form regional content (Andover, Derry, Portsmouth, Haverhill), claims ~$240M in 2022 volume and Top 1.5% of U.S. Realtors, and is syndicated in Boston Globe, HGTV, Forbes, USA Today, and Bankrate. Her site is an 8/10 — clean, editorial-leaning, conversion-optimized. What she does right that this build must match or beat: (1) commission transparency as a trust lever, (2) an actively maintained regional blog that anchors SEO, (3) saturated third-party press as proof, and (4) a newsletter-driven mailing machine (the "1–2 emails/week" the client flagged). What she does wrong that this build must exploit: her brand is fragmented across three domains (soldbylsg.com, northshorepropertyshop.com, newenglandpropertyshop.com), her headquarters and hero messaging are Andover-MA-first, and Southern NH towns are buried in blog tags — a Salem or Derry seller does not feel Sevajian is "their" agent on first glance. Karyn is.

The strategic imperative: **the site must make Karyn's local depth visible within three seconds of landing**. Not through her face — per the client briefing, face-forward branding is explicitly out — but through place. The one thing this build must do better than anything in the category is render **Salem, Windham, Derry, and Londonderry as the hero**: real photography, real neighborhood names, real property-tax numbers, real commute times, real school zones. Every competitor is selling "a realtor." Karyn should sell "the neighborhood, and the person in it who knows every street." This is the wedge — and it is currently unoccupied.

## 2. Target audience deep dive

### Primary persona — "Linda," seller (age 58–66, tight 62)

Linda has lived in Salem, Windham, or Pelham for 25–35 years. She raised two or three kids in a 3-bedroom colonial bought in the mid-1990s; the kids are now 28–34, working in Boston, Portsmouth, or Nashua, and just starting to have kids of their own. Her husband has recently retired or is about to. The house is "rattling around empty," the stairs are harder than they were, the lawn is a chore, and Derry/Salem/Windham property taxes keep climbing — a **resident review on Niche calls them "outragous"** and cites them as the reason the family "ended up moving out of state." Linda is considering a single-level condo at Tuscan Village in Salem or a townhouse near her first grandchild.

**Triggers (why she is moving now):** empty nest with visibly unused bedrooms; husband retired or recently widowed; first grandchild born, wanting to be within 20 minutes; house maintenance overwhelm (roof, taxes, lawn); equity unlock — the house is worth 3–4× what they paid and they want to fund retirement.

**Blockers (why she has not called yet):** fear the agent "won't know my neighborhood" (she's lived here 30+ years and expects the agent to know the cul-de-sac, the Canobie crowd, the Salem HS feeder lines); fear of being **pressured into a lowball price** — the FastExpert phrase "some realtors pressure you to sell below it's worth" is the articulation; fear of being **ghosted after the listing goes up** (the Zillow reviewer quote *"things seemed to change once she was guaranteed a paycheck"* is the archetype); emotional attachment to a "home of 43 years" (verbatim Jill & Co. testimonial language); and she does not yet know where she is moving *to*, so she is afraid of selling before she has a landing spot.

**Voice:** formal, grateful, relational. Uses "we," "my husband and I," "our family home." Full sentences. Values "professionalism," "patience," "kindness," being "walked through" the process.

### Secondary persona — "Mike & Jess," buyers (age 30–45, tight 32–38)

Mike and Jess are a dual-income couple, household income $160–220K, currently renting or in a starter condo in Methuen, Lawrence, Haverhill, Andover, or Woburn MA. One commutes to Boston two or three days a week; one is hybrid/remote. They have one child under 5 or are planning one. They have been watching Zillow obsessively for 18 months and are tired of getting outbid in MA. A Niche resident review captures their calculus exactly: *"Bought our first home here… shopping in Salem NH (15–20 min). And without traffic on the weekend it's only 45 minutes to Boston."*

**Triggers:** MA → NH **tax arbitrage** (no income tax, no sales tax, cheaper gas); pregnancy or a second child needing a yard, 3BR, garage; Boston commute still tolerable from Salem/Windham via I-93; school quality (Windham and Londonderry rank well on Niche); Massachusetts property-tax + income-tax fatigue.

**Blockers:** distrust of Zillow — a ConsumerAffairs reviewer writes *"the pictures on Zillow looks good but in person they look nothing like the pic"*; fear of overpaying in a bidding war ("multi-offer scenarios and bidding wars are the norm"); an agent who won't answer texts — they expect **11pm responses** (the exact Jill & Co. testimonial phrase *"texting me back at 11pm at night about silly questions"*); not knowing which NH towns are "real" (Is Derry sketchy? Is Salem just the mall? Is Windham worth the premium?); and NH-specific first-time buyer confusion around septic, well, and P&S contingencies.

**Voice:** direct, transactional, text-cadence. "Responsive," "knocked it out of the park," "got us," "first home," "under contract." Short sentences. High expectations on speed.

## 4. Pricing intelligence

**What the market anchors on.** NH is still a **percentage-commission** market. Clever's September 2025 survey puts the statewide average at **5.57% total**, split 2.90% listing / 2.67% buyer-side. FastExpert's agent poll puts NH lower at 4.7%. On a 2025 NH median sale price of **$496,656–$534,500** (Zillow / Houzeo), total commission runs **$27,000–$30,000** per transaction. Discount and flat-fee alternatives exist and are loud online — Redfin at "as low as 1%", Clever's NH partner at 1.5%, Houzeo at $249–$399, Flat Fee Group at ~$499, Brokerless at $99+ — but the traditional full-service channel has not meaningfully ceded share.

**How competitors frame pricing post-NAR settlement (Aug 17, 2024).** The settlement removed offers of compensation from the MLS and mandated written buyer-broker agreements before any tour. Year-one data actually shows buyer-agent commissions **ticking up**, not down: Redfin data published August 2025 shows buyer-side commissions rose from 2.38% to 2.43% nationally; homes under $500K now average 2.49–2.52%. The NH Business Review quotes NHAR President Ben Cushing's post-lawsuit mantra — *"disclose, disclose, disclose"* — but in practice, traditional NH agents (Verani, CB Dinsmore, Jill & Co., Lamacchia) **publish nothing** about commission on their sites. Sevajian is the only real outlier, disclosing a 2–6% range in a blog post. This is the category's uncomfortable tension: the rules and the rhetoric point at transparency; the websites don't.

**The psychological floor and ceiling.** Floor is the flat-fee MLS range (~$500) and Redfin's 1%. Ceiling is the traditional 6% "full service." **Middle is 2.5–3% per side** — the unspoken industry norm, and the number buyers and sellers actually encounter at the signing table.

**Where Karyn sits.** **Accessible premium.** Not the cheapest option, not the luxury-Compass option. Her market is Linda-the-downsizer and Mike-and-Jess-the-relocator, who are both price-sensitive but value-anchored — they will pay 2.5–3% to the right person, they will not pay 6% to anyone, and they will actively reject a 1% Redfin model because they have read enough Reddit and Zillow reviews to fear the ghost-after-signing pattern.

**Recommended pricing page strategy.** Do not publish a single flat number; do not hide the topic entirely. Lead with a **dedicated "How I'm paid" or "Commissions & the NAR changes" page** that (1) explains the post-August-2024 rules plainly, (2) discloses a **listing-side range (e.g., 2–3%)** and a **buyer-side range (e.g., 2.5–3%)** with the NAR-required "fully negotiable, not set by law" language, (3) frames the fee alongside the **services delivered for it** (professional photography, staging consult, 30–60 day listing agreement, etc.), and (4) addresses the Zillow/iBuyer defense head-on: cite the Real Estate Witch data that Opendoor takes roughly 9–14% below FMV plus fees (~$45K equity loss on a $500K home) and that Offerpad is worse. The page should lead with **value delivered**, anchor with **the range**, and close with **the iBuyer alternative cost comparison**. This is the single biggest **feature/content gap** in the category — it simultaneously signals trust, answers the post-NAR buyer's actual question, and captures high-intent "NH realtor commission" SEO that Clever, Real Estate Witch, and FastExpert currently monopolize.

## 7. Conversion patterns

**What converts in this category.** Across the five competitors, the conversion stack is nearly identical: (a) **home-valuation form** as the seller hook, (b) **IDX search bar** as the buyer hook, (c) **phone number in header** as the universal fallback, (d) **contact form** as the catch-all. Nobody embeds Calendly. Nobody offers same-day booking. The single biggest conversion-mechanic differentiator Karyn has is already in her brief: **inline Calendly in the hero**. That one decision puts her ahead of every competitor in the market on conversion friction.

**Trust signals — standard vs. differentiating.** Standard: written testimonials, years in business, transaction-count bragging, designations (ABR, CRS, GRI), brokerage affiliation. Differentiating: Verani's live "minutes ago" testimonial feed; Sevajian's saturated third-party press (Boston Globe, HGTV, Forbes); Sevajian's published commission range. For Karyn specifically: **her 32 existing testimonials, Zillow reviews that already use her target language** (*"breaks down confusing home buying jargon,"* *"puts the customer first"*), and **Jill & Co.'s historical Zillow Premier Agent partnership** are the differentiating proof. The site must surface these prominently — not hide them on a `/testimonials` page.

**Friction points — from real reviews.** *"Things seemed to change once she was guaranteed a paycheck"* (Zillow 1-star on a Londonderry agent); *"initially hired another real estate agent but found them to be somewhat unresponsive"* (Yelp Salem NH); *"didn't complete a comparative [market analysis]"* (Yelp Salem NH). The market's dominant complaint pattern is **responsiveness degradation after signing**. The second pattern is **weak CMA / lowball pricing pressure**. The third is **Zillow accuracy distrust** (*"pictures look nothing like the pic"* — ConsumerAffairs).

**Above-the-fold requirements (specific, no-scroll on mobile).**

1. Karyn's name + "Real Estate Agent, Jill & Co. — Salem NH" visible within 600px of the top, without a headshot dominating the frame.
2. Hero imagery is **original Southern NH photography** — Salem/Windham/Derry town centers, Cobbett's Pond, autumn clapboard, stone walls — **not** a Salem MLS thumbnail and **not** Karyn's face.
3. Primary CTA above the fold on mobile: "Book a free 15-minute consultation" opening the inline Calendly, not routed to a form.
4. Above-fold aggregate proof: star rating (from her 32 testimonials + Zillow reviews) and a single-line transaction or years-of-experience number.
5. Above-fold service-area line: "Serving Salem, Windham, Derry, Londonderry, Pelham, Atkinson, Hampstead."

**Friction points to eliminate.** Remove any contact form requiring more than three fields. Remove every "we'll get back to you within 24 hours" fallback — replace with a real-time Calendly slot. Eliminate pop-up lead magnets on first visit (they fire the Zillow-impersonal alarm for Mike and Jess). Remove any "Get your home's value instantly" claim that routes to a form promising later follow-up — either deliver the valuation on-page or reframe as "Request a real CMA, prepared personally."

## 8. Design & aesthetic landscape

**What you actually see on top 5 competitor sites.** Across Sevajian, Jill & Co., Verani, CB Dinsmore, and Lamacchia, the category runs on **the same four-ingredient recipe**: blue-or-cabernet + white palette; geometric sans-serif typography (Montserrat/Proxima/Gotham family, with Verani's BHHS serif the lone exception); photography composed of standardized studio headshots, MLS thumbnail feeds, and stock home exteriors; and a layout of sticky nav → hero image with IDX search overlay → featured-listings grid → agent grid → testimonials → blog teaser → fat footer.

**Color palette.** Four of five are blue-and-white variants — Coldwell Banker sea-blue, Lamacchia royal-blue gradient, Sevajian's medium-blue CTAs, Verani's navy-adjacent secondary. BHHS's cabernet red is the only real deviation. Red/coral and gold appear only as CTA buttons. **Nobody uses warm earth tones. Nobody uses greens despite New Hampshire being famous for foliage.**

**Typography.** Near-universal geometric sans. BHHS runs a brand serif on display; nobody else does. **Playfair, Cormorant, Freight, Canela — not present on any site in the set.** The editorial-serif voice is unoccupied.

**Photography.** Studio headshots in grids, MLS-feed thumbnails, generic couple-with-keys / family-on-porch stock. Verani uses the most landscape imagery but it still reads as stock. **Almost no drone, no genuine NH landscape photography, no lifestyle or editorial photography, no original photo art direction anywhere in the category.**

**Layout.** Template-driven on Ylopo (Sevajian), Delta Media / CB (Dinsmore), Tribus/Solid Earth (Jill & Co. IDX), and Elementor/WordPress (Lamacchia). Every site is a long-scroll, mobile-first, IDX-search-as-hero composition. Hero real estate is ceded to the property-search bar, not to brand or story.

**Visual differentiation opportunity.** Every Southern NH agent site is blue/white + geometric sans + headshot-grid + IDX-search-over-hero-house-photo. An **editorial, serif-led aesthetic** — Canela or Cormorant display paired with Inter or Söhne body — on a **warm New England palette** (cream, warm off-white, deep forest green or iron-oxide accent, black type) with **original landscape-first photography** (stone walls, clapboard colonials in autumn, Salem and Derry town centers, Canobie Lake light, birch and maple) would read as *boutique local expert with taste*. It is differentiated from Jill & Co.'s casual-script warmth, from Verani's franchise cabernet, from Lamacchia and Sevajian's blue SaaS template — without being so out-of-category that a 62-year-old seller finds it alien. The brokerage itself is described as boutique in its own copy; the website category that matches that positioning is currently *unoccupied*. This is the aesthetic wedge, and it happens to align perfectly with the client's "no heavy face-forward branding" directive: make the *place* the hero, not the person.

## 9. Strategic recommendations

### Do (5)

1. **Publish a transparent commission page** with listing-side (e.g., 2–3%) and buyer-side (e.g., 2.5–3%) ranges, the NAR-required "fully negotiable, not set by law" language, the value delivered for the fee, and a clear post-August-2024 explainer. Four of five competitors hide this entirely; Sevajian's 2–6% disclosure is her single strongest trust lever. Beat it.
2. **Build the MA → NH relocation hub as the flagship content pillar.** Calculator, tax math, commute times per town, DMV/inspection checklist, school comparison. This is the single biggest SEO and audience-capture opportunity in the market — no Southern NH agent owns it, and Mike and Jess are searching for it daily.
3. **Publish 8–12 neighborhood guides** (Salem, Windham, Derry, Londonderry, Pelham, Atkinson, Hampstead, plus sub-neighborhoods: Tuscan Village, Canobie Lake, Cobbett's Pond, Woodmont Commons) with real photography, price bands, school zones, and IDX integration. Earns every long-tail buyer query aggregators cannot answer locally.
4. **Embed Calendly inline in the hero and at the foot of every guide.** Nobody else in the market does this. It converts Mike and Jess in one click and removes the 24-hour-callback anxiety that drives Linda's biggest fear.
5. **Front-load real Zillow and Google review quotes verbatim** — Karyn's existing reviews already contain headline copy (*"incredibly knowledgeable about the Southern New Hampshire real estate market," "breaks down confusing home buying jargon," "puts the customer first"*). Use AggregateRating schema to surface these in SERPs.

### Avoid (3)

1. **Do not put Karyn's headshot in the hero or run a face-first brand system.** The client explicitly rejected it, and the category's reliance on headshot-grids is the cliché this site should step away from. Place Southern NH in the hero instead.
2. **Do not copy the blue-and-white IDX-search-over-house-photo template.** It is the category's visual commodity. A warm editorial palette with original landscape photography will read as boutique — matching Jill & Co.'s own self-description — without being disorientingly out of category.
3. **Do not pursue head terms like "homes for sale Salem NH" organically as a primary strategy.** Zillow, Redfin, Realtor.com, Trulia, Homes.com, and Movoto will outrank any new agent site for years. Invest organic effort in relocation, neighborhood, school-zoned, and commission-explainer content where aggregators are thin; use Google Business Profile and local-pack tactics for the head terms.

### Exploit (3)

1. **Exploit Sevajian's brand fragmentation and MA-first positioning.** She is the dominant email-marketing competitor and her content is strong, but her three domains (soldbylsg.com / northshorepropertyshop.com / newenglandpropertyshop.com) dilute her SEO, and her hero positioning is Andover-MA. A Salem or Derry seller who lands on Sevajian does not feel like she is theirs. Make Karyn unmistakably theirs within three seconds.
2. **Exploit the "sell my house Salem NH" keyword cluster.** Agents have ceded it to cash-buyer investors (We Buy Houses, Brendan Buys Houses, NH Home Buyers). A proper seller's guide with CMA and iBuyer-comparison content — citing the ~$45K equity loss on a $500K Opendoor offer documented by Real Estate Witch — can displace the cash-buyer SERP and capture sellers at their highest-intent moment.
3. **Exploit the downsizer emotional narrative for Linda.** She is not searching for "realtor." She is searching for "downsizing in New Hampshire," "selling the family home," "how to pick between selling first or buying first," and "property tax relief for seniors NH." Build a downsizer content pillar with elderly and veteran tax exemption data by town, a "sell-first vs. buy-first" walkthrough, and age-restricted community guides (The Fairways, Kings Court, Eden Estates, Artisan Flats). This is a high-conversion, low-competition wedge that speaks directly to Karyn's best-year-yet 2025 seller base.
